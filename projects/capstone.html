Overview

The problem with grid-scale batteries.

Grid-scale energy storage is dominated by electrochemical batteries — lithium-ion, mostly. They have real problems: rare-earth sourcing, capacity degradation with every cycle, thermal runaway risk, and end-of-life disposal issues.

Mechanical energy storage — compressed air (CAES) and pumped hydro (PHS) — dodges most of that. Long cycle life, no chemistry to degrade, conventional materials. But each has limitations on its own: CAES has fast response but lower round-trip efficiency; PHS has high efficiency but slow dynamics.

Our capstone is a sub-scale proof of concept that hybridizes the two. Both subsystems share a single 12V alternator shaft through overrunning clutches, with a PID controller managing RPM matching and voltage stability. If it works at this scale, the architecture scales up to grid-relevant sizes.

My Contribution

Controls Lead on a 6-person team.

I lead the electrical and controls side of the project — everything downstream of the mechanical shaft. That spans sensor selection and integration, alternator characterization, PID development on Arduino, and load-step testing.

The rest of the team handles mechanical design (shaft, clutches, turbines), fluid systems (pressure vessels, hydraulics), and the structural build of the 20 ft elevated water tank.

  • Alternator characterization. Ran torque-current analysis to size the drivetrain-generator coupling and inform PID tuning parameters.
  • PID voltage regulation. Built the Arduino-based closed-loop controller targeting 12V output within 5% under step-change loads.
  • Sensor integration. Specified and wired Hall effect current sensors and a voltage divider network for closed-loop feedback. Added software bounds checking to prevent PID instability from saturated ADC readings.
  • Testing. Designed load-step test procedures and instrumented the system for data collection during commissioning.
Technical Highlights

Key engineering decisions.

01

Shared alternator shaft with overrunning clutches

Both turbines (air + water) drive the same 12V alternator through independent overrunning clutches. This lets either subsystem generate power solo — or both at once in torque-sharing mode — without needing complex disengagement logic. The clutch handles the handoff mechanically.

02

PID tuning for dissimilar sources

CAES and PHS have very different response dynamics — pneumatic pressure changes fast, water flow changes slow. The controller has to maintain stable voltage output as either source comes online or drops out. I tuned the PID gains iteratively against simulated load profiles before validating on the physical rig.

03

ADC saturation handling

Arduino ADCs saturate at rail voltages. If the PID integrator keeps winding up when the signal is pegged high or low, recovery takes too long when conditions normalize. I added software bounds checking on the feedback input so the controller doesn't integrate against a saturated signal.

04

Hall effect + voltage divider feedback

Hall sensors measure alternator output current without needing a shunt in the power path — cleaner and safer at higher currents. A voltage divider network scales the 12V output down to the 0–5V Arduino ADC range. Both feed into the PID loop simultaneously.

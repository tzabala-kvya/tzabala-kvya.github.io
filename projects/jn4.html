Overview

Restoring a 100-year-old trainer.

The Curtiss JN-4 "Jenny" is one of the most historically significant American aircraft — the trainer most WWI pilots learned to fly on. The Flying Leatherneck Aviation Museum is restoring one to exhibit condition.

The challenge: the surviving archival drawings are incomplete, inconsistently scaled, and reference tolerances that don't exist on modern drawings. To rebuild the fuselage, the museum needed modern fabrication-ready CAD models — not just reference drawings.

My role was to engineer fuselage components in SolidWorks, assemble them into a full digital model, and organize the 3,000+ archival blueprints that 20 contributors were working from across the project.

My Contribution

CAD and organization.

  • Fuselage component modeling. Built SolidWorks models of fuselage components from legacy blueprints. Resolved tolerance ambiguity and missing dimensions by cross-referencing other drawings and physical reference parts where available.
  • Digital assembly. Produced a complete fuselage assembly that supports both in-house wood fabrication and outsourced metal parts manufacturing — mating features and interfaces are all tolerance-compatible.
  • Blueprint digitization pipeline. Streamlined the indexing of 3,000+ archival blueprints. Established a standardized file naming convention across 20 contributors to minimize search time and eliminate duplicate work.
Technical Highlights

Key engineering decisions.

01

Working from incomplete historical drawings

Pre-WWII engineering drawings don't use modern GD&T and often lack explicit tolerances. When a dimension was ambiguous, the process was: check if it matters for function (does this dimension affect fit?), find a mating drawing to triangulate, or reference the physical part if available. Every modeling decision was documented so other contributors could follow the reasoning.

02

Assembly that bridges wood and metal workflows

The JN-4 uses a mix of wood structural members and steel fittings. Wood gets fabricated in-house by museum volunteers; metal parts go to outsourced machine shops. The CAD assembly had to produce drawings that worked for both audiences — with clearances sized to accommodate hand-fitting on the wood side and precise machining on the metal side.

03

File naming for 20 contributors and 3,000+ drawings

When 20 people are indexing archival drawings, everyone invents their own naming convention. That's how files get lost. I established a standardized naming system (assembly-subassembly- component-revision) and onboarded contributors to it. Search time across the archive dropped dramatically and rework from duplicate drawings stopped.

Overview

Why stormwater biofiltration matters.

Every time it rains, urban runoff carries pollutants — heavy metals, PFAS compounds, sediment — off streets and parking lots into coastal waters. Biofiltration media (engineered soils, sand, carbon blends) are designed to trap those pollutants before they reach the ocean.

The question for stormwater engineers is: which media actually works, and for how long? Different media trap different pollutants at different rates, then eventually saturate. SCCWRP's research exists to quantify those tradeoffs so cities can pick the right media for their water chemistry and budget.

My work at SCCWRP spanned both sides of that: the hardware to produce repeatable rain for field tests, and the analytics to make sense of the sensor data that came out of those tests.

My Contribution

Hardware, analytics, and a paper.

  • Mobile rainfall simulator. Designed and calibrated the physical test rig. Target spec: a 0.87 uniformity coefficient — meaning water distribution across the test area is within 13% of the mean at every point. Achieved across soil, pavement, grass, and turf surfaces.
  • R analytics pipeline. Transformed multi-sensor storm-event data into infiltration rate and uniformity metrics. Showed that a 3-sensor setup captured the same trends as a 30-sensor array — cutting setup complexity and cost without sacrificing signal.
  • Breakthrough curve analysis. Analyzed how copper and PFAS/PFOA broke through different media types, quantifying exhaustion profiles and cost-to-performance tradeoffs for BMP media selection.
  • SOP authoring. Wrote standard operating procedures for infiltration and adsorption testing that were adopted by regional stormwater agencies.
  • Publication. Co-author on "Impact of Media Properties on Dissolved Copper Sorption in Stormwater Biofiltration," ES&T Water (2025).
Technical Highlights

Key engineering decisions.

01

Achieving 0.87 uniformity across mixed surfaces

Uniformity coefficient is the key quality metric for a rainfall simulator — if water falls harder on one side of the test plot, your runoff data is biased. Getting to 0.87 across soil, pavement, grass, and turf required calibrating nozzle pressure, height, and spacing for each surface's surface roughness and absorption behavior.

02

30 sensors → 3 sensors, same signal

Early field tests used ~30 sensors per plot to capture spatial variation in infiltration. Setup took hours. I built an R pipeline that cross-correlated the readings across every sensor position and identified 3 representative locations that captured the same trends as the full array. Field setup dropped from half a day to under an hour with no loss of resolution on the metrics we care about.

03

Breakthrough curves for media selection

Every media type has a saturation point — the moment it stops trapping a pollutant and starts letting it through. Breakthrough curves tell you when that happens and at what concentration. Analyzed these across several media types for both copper and PFAS/PFOA, which gave SCCWRP a quantitative basis for recommending media to stormwater agencies.

Overview

A powertrain that has to survive a racecar.

FSAE (Formula SAE) is a collegiate engineering competition where students design, build, and race open-wheel formula-style vehicles. UCI's team runs an electric platform.

The powertrain subteam is responsible for everything between the motor output and the wheels — mounting the motor, mounting the differential, sizing the final drive, and making sure it all survives the repetitive vibratory and torque loads of a racecar.

I joined as a Powertrain Engineer with responsibility for the mount design, FEA validation, gear ratio selection, and test-bench verification. The constraint was tight: mounts had to hold a 220 N·m drivetrain under dynamic loading while fitting inside the chassis packaging envelope — all designed for outsourced machining.

My Contribution

What I owned on the project.

  • Motor and differential mount design. CAD in SolidWorks, working from the motor and diff datasheets, chassis envelope constraints, and packaging coordination with the chassis subteam.
  • Structural validation. ANSYS FEA for static stress, torque loading, and vibratory/fatigue loads. Target: >1.5× safety factor on all load cases.
  • Final drive gear ratio. Selected a 3.3:1 ratio based on motor torque curve, target top speed, tire slip limits, and the available sprocket sizes that fit the packaging.
  • GD&T and DFM. Applied geometric tolerancing to all mount drawings so outsourced vendors could machine parts that actually fit on first articles — no in-house rework.
  • Test bench validation. Built a welded test fixture to load the assembled mounts under simulated operating conditions before the powertrain saw the car.
Technical Highlights

Key engineering decisions.

01

Targeting >1.5× safety factor across load cases

The mounts had to survive three very different load environments: static (holding the drivetrain weight), peak torque (220 N·m through the diff), and vibratory (cycle loading from the motor and road input). I ran each case independently in ANSYS and iterated the geometry until the lowest safety factor across all three crossed 1.5.

02

3.3:1 gear ratio for slip-limited acceleration

Lower ratios give you higher top speed. Higher ratios give you more torque at the tires. The ceiling isn't motor output — it's tire grip. Beyond a certain tractive force, the tires slip and you stop accelerating. 3.3:1 puts peak torque delivery right at the edge of that slip limit, given our tire/weight package.

03

GD&T so the parts arrive ready to assemble

Outsourced machining means you don't get a chance to iterate on tolerance issues — if the bolt pattern is off by 0.5 mm because the datum reference frame was ambiguous, the whole mount goes in the scrap bin. Every drawing used explicit datum references, position tolerance callouts, and surface profile controls on the mating faces.

04

Test-bench validation before vehicle integration

The simulation said the mounts would hold. I didn't trust that blind. I welded a test fixture that let me load the assembled mounts under representative torque and confirm no visible deflection or mounting bolt loosening. Every mount went through this before the car saw it.
